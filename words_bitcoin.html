In 2008, something remarkable happened.

Satoshi Nakamoto released Bitcoin: a system where strangers could exchange value
without trusting a bank, government, or any central authority.
For over a decade, Bitcoin has worked. Not because everyone is honest,
but because the system is designed to work even when people try to cheat.

But here's the problem Bitcoin solved:
How do you prevent double-spending without a central ledger-keeper?
If Alice and Bob both claim ownership of the same coin, who decides who's right?
Normally, you need a bank. But Bitcoin has no bank. So how does it work?

This is called the Byzantine Generals Problem. Imagine an army with generals
scattered across a battlefield. They need to coordinate an attack, but some generals
might be traitors. How do they reach consensus when they can't trust each other?
For decades, computer scientists thought this was impossible without a trusted authority.

But here's the genius insight: Game theory solves it.
Bitcoin doesn't require trust. It requires incentives that make honesty profitable
and dishonesty unprofitable. Why don't miners attack the network?
Why do nodes validate transactions they don't have to?
Because the rules reward cooperation and punish attacks.

Why Bitcoin's approach worked when others failed

Governments and banks solved double-spending with a ledger-keeper: a central authority
who decides which transactions are valid. Trust the bank, you trust the system.

But cryptographers wanted to do it without a central authority. They tried signatures, hashing, encryption.
None of it worked. Without a central timestamp, you can't prove which coin was spent first.

Bitcoin's breakthrough: A distributed ledger + game theory.

Instead of one ledger-keeper, there are thousands of nodes. Instead of requiring trust, Bitcoin requires proof-of-work + incentives.

• You can't rewrite the past (proof-of-work makes attacks expensive)
• You can't fake the majority (you need 51% hash power)
• You aren't incentivized to attack (honest mining is more profitable)
• The game is eternal (today's attack destroys tomorrow's rewards)

Imagine you're a miner wanting to send bitcoin. Two paths:

COOPERATE: You follow Bitcoin's consensus rules. You validate transactions honestly, include them in your block. The network accepts your block, you earn a reward. Everyone's transactions work. Network stays secure.

CHEAT: You ignore consensus rules. You accept invalid transactions or double-spend coins. You earn short-term profit, but other nodes reject your block. Your coins get orphaned.

Each participant faces the same choice. What should you do?

FULL NODE (Always-Cooperate): I validate everything according to Bitcoin's rules, period. No matter what you do. No matter the incentives. Pure consensus enforcement. But this naivety? It gets exploited by attackers.

ADAPTIVE NODE (Tester): First block: I validate honestly to test your response. Second block: I attempt a double-spend. If you reject it, I know you're serious about consensus—I cooperate forever. If you accept it, I know you're exploitable—I attack relentlessly.

But wait, consider this historical context:

Bitcoin has survived 15+ years without a centralized authority. Thousands of independent nodes validate transactions daily. But why? Individual miners could cheat. Any node could compromise.

The answer isn't universal honesty – it's better than that. Bitcoin's security comes from repeated interactions.

Every node interacts with the same network participants over and over. Every miner faces the same consensus rules repeatedly. Attack once, and your reputation is destroyed. Honest behavior is rewarded continuously.

Even before Bitcoin's formal design, communities using sound money independently discovered similar principles: repeated interaction builds trust.

They called it: the credibility & reputation system. You don't attack your peers when you know you'll face them tomorrow. And this works remarkably well!

Well, here's what's unique about Bitcoin: Unlike centralized systems where authority can change rules, nodes face the exact same consensus rules every block, forever.

It's an infinitely repeated game. And that changes everything.

Now, what if block propagation isn't instant?

(what does latency mean for the game? let's see...)

When block propagation is delayed, nodes don't all see the same chain at the same time. This creates an opportunity:

The race attack: You mine a block secretly. While others validate it, you start mining the next block yourself. When your block propagates, you might have a 2-block lead! This hidden mining (selfish mining) can be profitable if you have enough hash power.

If a node knows others will see its blocks with delay, it can exploit that delay by mining in secret. Suddenly, Always Cheat becomes profitable even against Copycat!

This is called: selfish mining.

If people can't see what you're doing, game theory shifts. Honest behavior is rewarded less. Selfish behavior is rewarded more.

Now the whole network spirals into distrust:

Because some miners can profit from selfish mining when latency is high, their share of the network grows. As dishonest miners become common, even honest miners feel pressured to attack to stay competitive.

And once everyone is attacking each other? Nobody can trust anybody. The network fractures. Different mining pools create different fork chains. Security degrades.

So our epidemic of distrust comes from changing the game structure. When participants can't observe each other's actions clearly, when delays create information asymmetry, when incentives reward hidden behavior... trust collapses, even among rational actors.

This is Bitcoin's core challenge: How do we design the game so that honesty remains profitable even when observation is imperfect?

Solutions include:

Faster blocks: Reduce latency so selfish mining is harder.

Stronger consensus rules: Make attacks visible & punishable.

Decentralized validation: More independent nodes catch attacks faster.

Better incentives: Reward behavior that strengthens the network, not just individual profit.

But these solutions create a tension: stronger consensus rules can be fragile.

Interestingly, Bitcoin's solution wasn't to centralize authority (which would solve the game but destroy decentralization). Instead, Bitcoin:

Made the rules transparent: Everyone agrees on the same consensus rules, globally, forever.

Made attacks visible: Any deviation from consensus is immediately detectable by anyone running a node.

Made punishment automatic: Invalid blocks are rejected. Cheaters gain nothing.

Made the game infinite: Miners face the same rules forever, so short-term attack gains don't justify long-term reputation loss.

By changing the game structure itself, Bitcoin made honesty the dominant strategy. Not because everyone is good, but because the rules make cheating unprofitable.

So now, the question becomes: How do we further strengthen these rules? How do we make attacks even more costly? How do we scale decentralization while maintaining game-theoretic security?

But there's another challenge: What if nodes make honest mistakes?

(what does noise (mistakes) do to the game?)

Here's the same network, but now there's a 1% chance each block is validated incorrectly (due to software bugs, hardware faults, network glitches, etc).

Oh no!

Naive Full Nodes, who always trust others' blocks, get tricked by mistakes into accepting invalid data. Their trust is punished by the network rejecting their chains.

But notice: Honest Nodes still survive!

Why? Because they don't just validate once – they maintain state. They catch mistakes when they see conflicting data, and they respond by being more defensive next time.

This is why Bitcoin has:

Full nodes running complete validation: Bitcoin nodes don't just trust—they verify every transaction, every signature, every rule. Every block is independently validated. This is why Bitcoin survived network delays, attacks, and bugs for 15 years.

Peer review of code changes: Any proposed change to Bitcoin's rules requires honest nodes to accept it. One node can't force an upgrade—the majority must agree. This is why hard forks require consensus, and why contentious changes get rejected (like the 2MB block size increase).

Consensus rules as the game's foundation: Blocks that violate consensus rules are rejected by the entire network, regardless of who mines them. This makes attacking unprofitable: your blocks get orphaned, your hash power is wasted, attackers eventually give up.

The lesson: Even with noise, the game works.

But only if: 1. Nodes validate independently 2. They respond to errors (don't just trust) 3. They maintain a shared record (consensus)

Robust systems aren't built on perfect actors. They're built on game theory that survives imperfect information.

Honest Nodes win when:

...the reward for mutual honesty (R) is high enough compared to temptation (T).

...the network can punish cheaters fast enough that they don't accumulate wealth.

...honest nodes outnumber dishonest ones initially, OR the rules make dishonesty unprofitable from the start.

...the game is repeated infinitely, so short-term gains don't outweigh long-term losses.

This is the core insight behind Bitcoin's design:

• Block rewards (R): Bitcoin gives miners 6.25 BTC per honest block. This makes cooperation profitable.
• Difficulty adjustment (T): Attacks are expensive—you need 51% of the network's hash power. Most attackers can't afford it.
• Consensus rules (P): Invalid blocks are rejected. Even if you control 51% hash power, you can't force the network to accept violations.

These aren't accident. They're designed to make honesty the dominant strategy.

So here's the critical insight: Trust isn't about believing people are good. It's about designing systems where honesty is the only profitable strategy.

Bitcoin achieves this through game theory, not through a central authority. When you change the payoffs (increase block rewards, lower mining difficulty, alter consensus rules), you change which strategies win. Honest nodes dominate when honesty is rewarded. Attackers emerge when attacks are underpriced.

This is why governance matters. The community must actively maintain the game structure that makes Bitcoin work.

So here's what we learned:

Single transactions create a prisoner's dilemma: rational actors cheat.

Repeated games change everything: honest strategies win because players face each other forever.

Tournament evolution shows: even when cheaters start strong, honesty dominates long-term.

Changing the game (adding delays, noise, attacks) can make trust collapse...

...but stronger consensus rules, transparency, & verification can restore it.

Bitcoin applies these principles:
• Global consensus rules: Every node validates the same way (the game is always the same)
• Transparent validation: All transactions are public (everyone sees if you cheat)
• Automatic punishment: Invalid blocks are rejected by the entire network (attackers can't win)
• Infinite repetition: Bitcoin runs forever (so attacking today destroys tomorrow's profits)
• Decentralized participation: Thousands of independent nodes (no single authority to bribe)

The result: a system where millions of strangers maintain a shared ledger without trusting a central authority.

It works not because everyone is honest – but because honesty is the only profitable strategy. An attacker might gain short-term advantage, but the network punishes them, reputation spreads, and honest nodes dominate long-term. This is why Bitcoin survived 15+ years without being hacked or requiring central control.

That's the evolution of trust.
